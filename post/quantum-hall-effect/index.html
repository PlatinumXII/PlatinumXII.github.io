QHE Part I: Classical Hall Effect

1 Classical Hall Effect, the Basic Version

Set up:

Fig.1 Classical Hall effect, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hall_effect.

Physics:

Hall voltage:

Due to the Lorentz force, the moving electrons inside the conductor will be forced to move and accumulate to the left side in the presence of magnetic field.

(1.1)mdv⃗dt=−ev⃗×B⃗m\frac{\rm{d}\vec{v}}{\rm{d}t} = -e \vec{v}\times \vec{B}\tag{1.1} mdtdv​=−ev×B(1.1)

This leaves equal and opposite charges exposed on the other face, where there is a scarcity of mobile charges.

The separation of charge establishes an electric field that opposes the migration of further charge, so a steady electric potential is established for as long as the charge is flowing. Then, we want the following equations to hold in equilibrium:

FE=FBeVHw=evxBzVHw=IxBznetwF_E = F_B\\ e\frac{V_H}{w} = e v_x B_z\\ \frac{V_H}{w} = \frac{I_xB_z}{netw} FE​=FB​ewVH​​=evx​Bz​wVH​​=netwIx​Bz​​

where Ix=(−e)(−vx)n⋅twI_x = (-e)(-v_x)n\cdot twIx​=(−e)(−vx​)n⋅tw and twtwtw is the cross-sectional area shown on the figure. Hence, the induced Hall voltage is then

VH=IxBznetV_H = \frac{I_xB_z}{net} VH​=netIx​Bz​​

We then consider the case electrons are restricted in (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) plane. And we specify that B⃗\vec{B}B pointing the zzz direction and current III is made to flow in xxx direction.

The equation of motion (1.1)(1.1)(1.1) becomes:

{mx¨=−ey˙Bmy¨=ex˙B\begin{cases} m\ddot{x} = -e\dot{y}B\\ m\ddot{y} = e\dot{x}B \end{cases} {mx¨=−ey˙​Bmy¨​=ex˙B​

2 Drude Model

Assumptions:

  • Independent electron approximation: the Drude model neglects long-range interaction between the electron and the ions or between the electrons and neglects the coliisions between electrons as well;
  • Free electron approximation: the only interaction of a free electron with its environment was treated as being collisions with the impenetrable, fixed ions core and the electrons move in straight lines between one collision and another;
  • Collision assumption: the collision is simultaneous. And after a collision event, the distribution of the velocity and direction of an electron is determined by only the local temperature and is independent of the velocity of the electron before the collision event. The electron is considered to be immediately at equilibrium with the local temperature after a collision;
  • Relaxation time: the average time between subsequent collisions of such an electron is τ\tauτ, with a memoryless Poisson distribution.

Physics:

Equation of motion:

We here apply both electric field and magnetic field leading to the generic force

f⃗(t)=(−e)(E⃗+v⃗×B⃗)\vec{f}(t) = (-e)\left(\vec{E} + \vec{v}\times\vec{B}\right) f​(t)=(−e)(E+v×B)

and we do the time varying analysis for the equation of motion:

p⃗(t0+dt)=(1−dtτ)[p⃗(t0)+f⃗(t0)dt+O(dt2)]+dtτ[g⃗(t0)+f⃗(t0)dt+O(dt2)]\vec{p}(t_0+{\rm{d}} t) = \left(1-\frac{{\rm{d}}t}{\tau}\right)\left[\vec{p}(t_0) + \vec{f}(t_0){\rm{d}}t + O({\rm{d}}t^2)\right] + \frac{{\rm{d}}t}{\tau} \left[\vec{g}(t_0) + \vec{f}(t_0){\rm{d}}t + O({\rm{d}}t^2)\right] p​(t0​+dt)=(1−τdt​)[p​(t0​)+f​(t0​)dt+O(dt2)]+τdt​[g​(t0​)+f​(t0​)dt+O(dt2)]

where g⃗\vec{g}g​ is the momentum after collision and on average will come out in a random direction and will contribute nothing. We drop the terms of order dt2{\rm{d}}t^2dt2, then arrive the desired equation of motion:

(2.1)mdv⃗dt=−e(E⃗+v⃗×B⃗)−mv⃗τm\frac{\rm{d}\vec{v}}{\rm{d}t} = -e\left(\vec{E} + \vec{v}\times\vec{B}\right) - m\frac{\vec{v}}{\tau}\tag{2.1} mdtdv​=−e(E+v×B)−mτv​(2.1)

Conductivity:

We are interested in equilibrium solutions of (2.1)(2.1)(2.1), when dv⃗dt=0\displaystyle\frac{\rm{d}\vec{v}}{\rm{d}t}=0dtdv​=0:

(2.2)v⃗+eτmv⃗×B⃗=−eτmE⃗\vec{v} +\frac{e\tau}{m}\vec{v}\times\vec{B} = -\frac{e\tau}{m}\vec{E}\tag{2.2} v+meτ​v×B=−meτ​E(2.2)

We know the current density J⃗\vec{J}J is related to velocity v⃗\vec{v}v by

J⃗=nev⃗\vec{J} = ne\vec{v} J=nev

Where nnn is the density of electrons. In matrix notation, (2.2)(2.2)(2.2) becomes

(1ωBτ−ωBτ1)J⃗=e2nτmE⃗\begin{pmatrix} 1&\omega_B \tau\\ -\omega_B \tau&1 \end{pmatrix} \vec{J} = \frac{e^2 n \tau}{m}\vec{E} (1−ωB​τ​ωB​τ1​)J=me2nτ​E

we invert this matrix:

J⃗=e2nτm⋅11+ωB2τ2(1−ωBτωBτ1)E⃗≡σDC1+ωB2τ2(1−ωBτωBτ1)E⃗≡σE⃗\vec{J} = \frac{e^2n\tau}{m}\cdot \frac{1}{1+\omega_B^2\tau^2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\omega_B \tau\\ \omega_B \tau &1 \end{pmatrix}\vec{E} \equiv \frac{\sigma_{DC}}{1+\omega_B^2\tau^2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\omega_B \tau\\ \omega_B \tau &1 \end{pmatrix}\vec{E}\equiv\sigma\vec{E} J=me2nτ​⋅1+ωB2​τ21​(1ωB​τ​−ωB​τ1​)E≡1+ωB2​τ2σDC​​(1ωB​τ​−ωB​τ1​)E≡σE

which is the Ohm's Law and σ\sigmaσ is the conductivity tensor while σDC\sigma_{DC}σDC​ is the DC(direct current) conductivity in the absense of a magnetic field.

The off-diagonal terms are responsible for the Hall effect: in equilibrium, a current in the xxx-direction requires an electric field with a component in the yyy-direction to cancel the bending due to the magnetic field.

Resistivity:

The resistivity is defined as the inverse of the conductivity:

ρ=σ−1=(ρxxρxy−ρxyρyy)\rho = \sigma^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho_{xx} & \rho_{xy}\\ -\rho_{xy} & \rho_{yy} \end{pmatrix} ρ=σ−1=(ρxx​−ρxy​​ρxy​ρyy​​)

ρ=1σDC(1ωBτ−ωBτ1)\rho = \frac{1}{\sigma_{DC}}\begin{pmatrix} 1&\omega_B \tau\\ -\omega_B \tau&1 \end{pmatrix} ρ=σDC​1​(1−ωB​τ​ωB​τ1​)

Recall the value of ωB\omega_BωB​ and σDC\sigma_{DC}σDC​, we can calculate:

ρxx=mne2τ and ρxy=Bne\rho_{xx} = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau} \text{ and }\rho_{xy} = \frac{B}{ne} ρxx​=ne2τm​ and ρxy​=neB​

and ρxx→0\rho_{xx}\to0ρxx​→0 as scattering processes become less important and τ→∞\tau\to\inftyτ→∞.

Hall coefficient:

Hall coefficient is defined by

RH=−EyJxB=ρxyBR_H = -\frac{E_y}{J_x B} = \frac{\rho_{xy}}{B} RH​=−Jx​BEy​​=Bρxy​​

in Drude model:

RH=1neR_H = \frac{1}{ne} RH​=ne1​

which is independent of τ\tauτ.

We can then say: Hall resistance/coefficient/resistivity measures the density of extended regions that DO conduct.

3 Hall Effect Has No Geometric Knowledge/Sensitivity

We recall the process of calculating Hall effect, i.e., ρxy\rho_{xy}ρxy​, we can tell that the Hall effect is not sensitive to the geometry of the sample. However, the longitudinal resistivity or the longitudinal resistance are dependent of the geometry.
